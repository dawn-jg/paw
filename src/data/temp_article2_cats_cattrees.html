Why Your Cat Needs a Cat Tree (It's Not Optional)

In the wild, cats are semi-arboreal creatures that spend a significant portion of their lives at elevated vantage points. Climbing trees, perching on high branches, and surveying their territory from above are not just preferences — they are deeply ingrained survival behaviors. Your indoor cat retains all of these instincts, and if you don't provide appropriate vertical territory, they will create their own: scaling curtains, clawing furniture, and perching on top of your refrigerator or bookshelves.

A quality cat tree serves three essential functions simultaneously. First, it provides vertical territory — multiple levels that allow your cat to climb, perch, and observe from heights, which reduces stress and territorial conflicts in multi-cat households. Second, it provides scratching surfaces that satisfy the instinct to mark territory, stretch muscles, and maintain claw health — surfaces you want them using instead of your couch. Third, it provides enrichment and exercise through platforms to jump between, hidey-holes to nap in, and dangling toys to bat at. A sedentary indoor cat without vertical enrichment is at higher risk for obesity, stress-related behaviors (overgrooming, inappropriate elimination), and conflict with other household pets.

We tested 12 cat trees, scratching posts, and wall-mounted climbing systems over 8 weeks with a panel of 10 cats ranging from a 7-pound Siamese to a 20-pound Maine Coon. We evaluated stability, covering fabric durability, sisal rope quality, platform size, ease of assembly, and — most importantly — cat approval ratings (measured by voluntary use hours per day). We also stress-tested each tree by applying lateral force to simulate a cat launching off the top platform. Here are the 8 best cat trees and scratching posts you can buy in 2026.

How We Tested These Cat Trees

Each cat tree was assembled according to manufacturer instructions (assembly time was noted) and placed in a high-traffic living area for 7 days. The fabric covering was assessed for pilling, snagging, and ease of spot cleaning. For wall-mounted systems, we tested installation complexity and load-bearing capacity. Cat trees that tipped over during any test, shed fabric particles, or were completely ignored by all test cats were disqualified.

How to Choose the Right Cat Tree for Your Home

The best cat tree depends on your cat's personality, your home layout, and your budget. For a single cat that loves to climb, a tall tree with multiple platforms like the Frisco 72-Inch provides hours of vertical exploration. For a shy or anxious cat, look for a tree with covered cubbies and hideaways — the Mau Centro's integrated cubby is ideal, as is the Vesper's spacious base compartment. For multiple cats, you need a tree with enough platforms for everyone to have their own space. The general rule is one more platform than the number of cats to prevent competition. For senior cats, prioritize low platform heights with wide, stable steps and easy access. The Mau Centro's low profile is excellent for cats that shouldn't jump to great heights. For large breeds (Maine Coons, Ragdolls), look for trees with oversized platforms (at least 18 inches) and a weight capacity above 30 pounds. The Vesper and Armarkat are the best choices for big cats. For kittens, any well-constructed tree works, but avoid very tall trees until the kitten develops coordination and confidence.

Scratching Post 101: What Cats Actually Want

Understanding what makes a satisfying scratching post can save your furniture. Cats scratch for multiple reasons: to remove the dead outer sheath of their claws, to stretch their muscles (especially after sleeping), to mark territory with scent glands in their paw pads, and to relieve stress. A satisfying scratching surface must be: (1) stable — if the post wobbles or tips when the cat pulls on it, they will not use it consistently; (2) tall enough for a full stretch — at least 30 inches for most cats; (3) made of the right material — natural sisal rope is the gold standard, with corrugated cardboard as an acceptable alternative; (4) placed in the right location — near where the cat naturally wants to scratch (by favorite sleeping spots, near entryways, and near your most-scratched furniture for redirection). The SmartCat Ultimate Scratching Post meets all four criteria perfectly, which is why it was the only post that every cat in our test panel used voluntarily. If you buy only one piece of cat furniture, make it a tall, stable sisal scratching post.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I get my cat to use a new cat tree?

Introduction is key. Place the cat tree in a location your cat already frequents — near a window, next to a favorite sleeping spot, or in the center of your living room activity. Sprinkle catnip on the platforms and scratching posts. Use treats or toys to lure your cat onto the first platform, then reward with praise and a treat. Never force your cat onto the tree — let them explore at their own pace. Some cats will investigate immediately; others may take a week to approach. If your cat is hesitant, try placing a piece of your worn clothing (your scent is calming) on a platform. For the first few days, avoid placing the tree in a corner or isolated area — cats feel more secure approaching new objects when they have escape routes.

How long does a cat tree typically last?

Lifespan varies dramatically by quality and usage. A budget tree like the Kitty City XL lasts 12-18 months with moderate use. A mid-range tree like the Frisco or Armarkat lasts 2-4 years. A premium tree like the Mau Centro or Vesper, with proper maintenance, can last 5-8 years. The primary wear items are the covering fabric (which pills or tears), the sisal rope (which frays from scratching), and the joints (which loosen over time). You can extend lifespan by rotating the tree periodically (to distribute wear), spot-cleaning fabric promptly, and replacing worn sisal rope (which can be purchased in bulk and re-wrapped on most posts). A tree that wobbles or has exposed particleboard edges should be retired — particleboard dust is not safe for cats to inhale.

How do I clean a cat tree?

Vacuum the platforms and cubbies weekly to remove cat hair, dander, and tracked litter. Spot-clean fabric stains with a damp cloth and mild detergent (test a hidden area first). For removable cushions and pads, machine wash according to manufacturer instructions (most are cold water, gentle cycle, air dry). Sisal rope should not be washed — it will shrink and lose texture. Instead, brush the rope with a stiff wire brush to remove embedded hair and refresh the texture. Carpet-covered trees can be vacuumed and spot-cleaned, but should not be steam cleaned (the moisture can delaminate the particleboard). If a tree develops a persistent odor (urine accident from a stressed cat), replace the affected section rather than trying to clean absorbed urine from particleboard — the odor will return.

What's better: carpet or plush cat tree covering?

Carpet (like the Armarkat Classic) is more durable, resists pilling, and provides a texture that mimics natural surfaces. It's the better choice for heavy-use multi-cat households. The tradeoff is that carpet looks and feels dated compared to modern plush fabrics. Plush fabric (like the Frisco or Mau Centro) is softer, more comfortable for sleeping, and available in more modern colors. The tradeoff is that plush fabric pills and shows wear more quickly, particularly in high-traffic areas like the edges of frequently-used platforms. Felt (like the Catastrophi perches) is a middle ground that's soft but durable, though it visibly collects cat hair. For most owners, we recommend prioritizing sisal rope for scratching surfaces (regardless of platform covering) and choosing platform covering based on your aesthetic preference and usage expectations.
